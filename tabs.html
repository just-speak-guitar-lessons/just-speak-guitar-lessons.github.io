Tablature, often just called "tabs," is a really cool way to learn and play your favorite songs on the guitar. It's like a cheat sheet for guitar, or ukelele players!

Tabs are made up of six lines that represent the six strings of your guitar. The top line represents the thinnest string (the high E string on a guitar), and the bottom line represents the thickest string (usually the low E string). Heres a quick video to explain further.

To open the Voice Commands, just say, "Connie, Open the Command Hub."

Now, instead of traditional sheet music with notes, tabs use numbers. These numbers tell you which fret to press down on and which string or strings to play. For example, if you see numbers in a vertical row, it always means you play a chord at this point. Usually, a chord diagram is placed above this row of numbers to signify this, although these diagrams are not always present.

When you see a single number present, this means to pluck this individual string. For example, seeing a "0" means to play that string open and likewise, a "3" means you should press down the third fret on that string. Easy, right?

The Symbols

In guitar tabs, there are some common symbols or notations that help convey various techniques and instructions on what to do with the strings. Here are some of the most frequently used symbols:


Numbers

Fret Numbers: On each string line, you'll see numbers either stacked vertically or on their own. These numbers indicate the fret where you should press down on the string and plat to produce the desired note. For example, if you see a "3" on the A string, it means you should press down the third fret on A string. The diagram above demonstrates how chords are tabbed and should be strummed.

Open and Closed strings

"X" and "O": When you see an "X" on a string in a guitar tab, it usually means that you should mute or deaden that string. You don't play the string open or fret any notes on it. This can also have the "P.M" which means "Palm Mute." "O" (Open String): When you see an "O" on a string in a guitar tab, it indicates that you should play that string open, without pressing down on any frets.

Hammers and Pulls

"h" (Hammer-On): The "h" between two notes, means you should play the first note by plucking the string and then, without picking it again, quickly press down on a higher-numbered fret. "p" (Pull-Off): "p" between two notes means you should play the first note by plucking the string and then release the higher-numbered fret to produce the second note.

Bending

"b" (Bend): The "b" or arrow symbol is usually followed by a number that indicates how far you should bend the note. Bend notations include "Â½" (a half-step bend, which should replicate the note at the next fret), "1" (a whole-step bend which should replicate the note that is 2 frets ahead), or other specific values like "2" or "3," depending on the desired pitch change.

Vibrato

"~" (Vibrato): This symbol is usually placed above or below a sustained note, indicating that you should apply vibrato to that note. You typically play the note as you normally would, and then apply a slow bend up and down until the note dissipates. This adds a subtle wavering or shimmering effect. Vibrato is a crucial technique for adding expression, emotion, and a vocal-like quality to guitar solos.

Sliding

"/" (slide): The Slide symbol is placed between two fret numbers on the same string. The first fret number represents the starting point of the slide, and the second fret number represents the ending point. Slides are commonly used in guitar playing to add smooth transitions between notes, create a sense of movement in a melody or solo. Its important to press down fully.

Guitar tab is widely used by guitarists to learn and play songs without requiring extensive knowledge of traditional sheet music notation.
