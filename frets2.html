Finger Positioning

Playing the guitar can be a very rewarding, healthy and a satisfying skill. It can never be mastered because there is always something new to learn! Once a guitarist learns how to hold and tune their guitar properly, the next step is learning finger posture and positioning. Posture is important in all aspects of life, such as sitting in your chair or standing for example. If your posture is right then you will perform to the best of your ability, its pretty simple, if your postures good - you play good.

So in this lesson we are going to learn about Finger Positioning. One way of retaining all of these skills is by learning the numbers of each finger rather than their names. More on that later, for now we'll just look into how to start.

For all voice commands for this website, just say, "Open Voice Commands," at any time to open the Help Box.

You are going to be moving your fingers into new positions playing the guitar, a good technique is before and whilst playing the guitar to stretch your fingers across and off the fretboard, this stops your finger's and wrist's from cramping too.

Finger Positioning

The is a chord diagram. The vertical lines are the strings. The horizontal lines are the metal strips going across your fretboard, these are called frets.

The black dots represent were to place your fingers to play the chord. This chord is called E-minor, the numbers within these dots represent which finger to use.

Each number represents a finger, the Index finger in number 1, the Middle is number 2, the Ring finger is number 4 and your little finger is number 4. Occasionally you will use your thumb, because it is not a finger, it is labelled simply with the letter, "T."

Finger Posture

The image above displays how to position your fingers to play the E Minor chord(as pictured in the chord box above), using the fingers also described in the hand image. If you pay close attention you will see that the Guitarist's finger tips are as close to the fret (horizontal metal strip on the Fret Board) as possible. The player's hand is arched across the fret board so that only the tips of hes playing hand's finger's are touching the designated places.

This is the perfect way to play a chord, you can hear if you aren't fretting the chord correctly as you will hear an undesirable buzzing sound or the strings will sound muted. If you do hear the string's buzzing then press harder on the strings and if strings sound muted then you need to arch your hand off the strings more. To do this imagine a small ball is in the palm of your hand, that is only your playing fingers should touch the strings, not your palm or any other part's of your fingers.

This will take some time time to get used to, if you feel any aches in your wrist then stops and rest for a few hours and then try again.

Correctly fretting a chord will take time, practice makes perfect! Never give up, remember to rest and then come back to it later. Just remember the phrase Every Amateur Does Get Better Eventually.
