To Start with . . .

Lead guitar is the musical part of a song were the guitarist picks the chords plays along melodically with the rest of the group and play's an instrumental were there may be a gap between verses, known as a solo. This is were the guitarist would play a group of notes named a, "riff."

All of this builds up within the song structure and then explodes into the solo part of the song. The solo is usually the section of a song that is the inspiration for a anyone deciding to learn the guitar.

The Electric Guitar

The solo of any song can also be played on an acoustic guitar, but it is the electric guitar that releases those tones, effects and distortion we are all familiar with. There are many variations of an electric guitar but they all work off the same principal. Electro-Magnetic pick ups. The guitar itself has minimal electricity running through it, so it would be impossible to give the player an electric shock but it does happen to connect to an amplifier which enables the sound to travel through to a speaker. Given this extra affordance many sound effects can be achieved which is what makes this instrument so versatile and popular through out the years and probably a lot more.

The need for an Electric Guitar surfaced as orchestras' started to increase in size, they also became much louder. The 1920's Big Band's achieved their sound through heavy drum beats and brass instruments and the acoustic guitar couldn't be heard over the sound. Before the invention of the pick up musicians would use microphones from old telephones, of which, usually broke under the constant usage and damage.

The first electric guitar was invented in 1931 by George Beauchamp and Adolf Rickenbacker and later patented in 1937 under the Rickenbacker name.
